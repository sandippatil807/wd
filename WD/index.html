Helping the Needy to Stand On their feet....

Caring

Witness the growth spurt in non-governmental organizations (NGOs) and you would be forgiven for thinking the world becomes a more caring place every day..Focusing on alleviating poverty and social exclusion. We do this through well-planned and comprehensive programmes in health, education, livelihoods and disaster preparedness and response. We are helping frontline workers stay safe with distributing dry rations to feed the needy, raising awareness in our programme areas.The common factor uniting this group, apart from the fact that they were neither government agencies nor businesses in the traditional sense, is that they would have an avowed mission to work for a social good – whether it was as torchbearers for human rights, the environment or just old-fashioned ‘development’

Along with governments and corporations, the two torrents of power in the global landscape, NGOs are seen as a third force. Indeed, the big international ones – the BINGOs – with budgets of hundreds of millions of dollars are pretty powerful. But are they a countervailing force, striving tirelessly for social justice and the underdog? Poverty alleviation may be the rhetoric, critics argue, but in practice little that is lasting has been achieved on this front by NGO activism.

Spreading Awareness

The social and non-government agencies can help the country to fight this situation by spreading awareness among people and guiding them instead of creating panic. Proper and healthy instructions are required to get the country through the fear of Covid-19. Regular scanning should be done at railway stations, bus stands and airports. No foreigner should be allowed to enter the country. People should stay home for as much time as possible. It should be taken care that no fake messages should be forwarded through social media as it will create panic among people. Only government notices and messages should be forwarded. Panic will only create stress and it is the need of the hour to keep calm and be aware.

According to the World Health Organisation (WHO), more than 11,000 people have died all over the world due to coronavirus and thousands have been suspected to succumb to the disease. There is a strong need to tackle this crisis. For this, NGOs can help the government by providing them financial support. Many social organisations can also aware general public about how to prevent themselves from the virus. Charitable hospitals should provide the free medical treatment and medicine to public and guide them to follow the rules and guidelines issued by the government to curb this crisis.

Social and non-government organisations can help the government of India by allocating among themselves areas to be supervised by them. They must ensure in their specified locality’s grocery stores that customers are not hoarding essential commodities and should try to prevent panic-buying. They must also check the availability of masks and sanitisers at medical store and that they are not selling the same at exorbitant prices.

There is growing anxiety about the potential dire consequences of the virus. Government should order to close non-essential business. They should work towards helping families that might be affected by closure of businesses and possible layoffs. NGOs should offer assistance to those in need and open up portals for those who want to help to donate masks, hand sanitisers and other essential commodities. Awareness videos should be made by NGOs to teach people about the virus, its symptoms and precautions. Proper screening of people should be done before entering crowded public places such as malls.

In this technical and modern world, everything has become advanced. In such times, it becomes our responsibility to deal with such a deadly virus intelligently and sensibly. Social media influencers can aware people to be safe and must maintain cleanliness at homes and around their surroundings. NGOs should provide the knowledge of how to keep themselves safe while sneezing and coughing. The organisations should spread awareness among the illiterate and rural communities on how to be safe at such times.

Social and non-government organisations could play a positive role to aware and protect people. The government is issuing various measures and advisories to contain the spread of coronavirus. But to properly educate masses at ground level about but to dos and don’ts at work place and home or during travelling, no one can do it better than these organisations. NGOs may organise small seminars, especially in marginalised colonies, where people have limited access to mass media, to aware them and conduct health check ups as well. As situation is alarming by the day, it is clear that panic among public may surge. NGOs can help the government to calm people by using local social connections.

The term ‘non-government organisation’ includes various categories of entities that operate not to obtain financial gain, and also do not belong to government sector. NGOs are an integral part of a democratic society. Sociological sciences lay emphasis on their importance in enhancing social integration, implementation of the principle of subsidiarity, building civil society, social dialogue and participatory democracy. The main tasks of NGOs in the health system are providing services and health advocacy. Provision of services includes medical, social and psychological services as well as, integration activities, care and nursing, material and financial support, educational and information services and training. Health advocacy is a combination of individual and social actions designed to gain political commitment, policy support, social acceptance and systems support for a particular health goal or program. An important task carried out by NGOs is participation in the formation of health policy. The increasing role of NGOs in providing social services and the participation in political processes, result in the need to confirm the validity and credibility of their operation. One of the ways could be to introduce the mechanisms to assess quality and efficiency, such as registration as a part of a legal system, self-regulatory activities (card rules, codes of ethics), certification, participation in networks, monitoring and audit.
